📖 How to Write a Peer-Quality Literature Review

A literature review is a critical synthesis of existing research on your topic. It is not a list of summaries — it is an argument about the state of knowledge in your field. Committee members and journal reviewers will evaluate your ability to critically engage with the literature, not just report it.

The 5-Step Framework

  • 1. Define scope: Choose your time range, geographic focus, and key concepts. Use Boolean operators in Scopus, Web of Science, PubMed, or Google Scholar.
  • 2. Organize by themes: Group studies thematically, not chronologically. What major debates, theories, and methodological approaches exist?
  • 3. Map the conversation: Who agrees? Who disagrees? What are the main theoretical frameworks competing in this space?
  • 4. Identify the gap: This is the most critical part. Your research must fill a genuine gap. Justify why your study is needed now.
  • 5. Write critically: Use critical language: "While X claims... Y challenges this by arguing..." "A consistent limitation across studies is..." "Notably absent from the literature is..."

Common Literature Review Mistakes

  • Writing descriptive summaries instead of critical synthesis
  • Ignoring contradictory findings or competing theories
  • Failing to connect the literature to your specific research gap
  • Using outdated sources beyond your chosen time window
  • Organizing chronologically rather than thematically
