⚖️ Complete IRAC Method Guide for Law Students & Attorneys

The IRAC method (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion) is the gold standard framework for legal analysis used in law schools, bar exams, and professional legal practice worldwide. Mastering IRAC is essential for anyone studying or practicing law.

The IRAC Framework — Explained

  • I — Issue: Identify the precise legal question the court must answer. State it as a question. Be specific: not "Was there a contract?" but "Did the parties form a valid contract when the offeree responded by email 48 hours after the offer's stated deadline?"
  • R — Rule: State the applicable legal rule, statute, or precedent. Include the legal standard and any elements that must be satisfied. Cite cases or statutes accurately.
  • A — Application (Analysis): Apply the rule to the facts. This is the most important and longest section. Analyze each element of the rule against the specific facts. Address both sides' arguments.
  • C — Conclusion: State your conclusion on the issue. Answer the question posed in the Issue section directly and definitively.

FIRAC vs IRAC — What's the Difference?

FIRAC adds a detailed Facts section at the beginning, making it more suitable for complex cases where the facts themselves are disputed or central to the analysis. FIRAC is preferred in many UK and Commonwealth law schools.

IRAC StructureIssue → Rule → Application → Conclusion
FIRAC StructureFacts → Issue → Rule → Application → Conclusion
CREAC StructureConclusion → Rule → Explanation → Application → Conclusion (restate)

IRAC in Criminal Law vs Civil Law

In criminal law, the Issue typically focuses on whether the defendant possessed the required mens rea (mental intent) and committed the actus reus (guilty act). The Rule section cites the relevant criminal statute and leading cases. The Application analyzes the prosecution's and defense's positions.

In civil law (contracts, torts, property), the Issue identifies which elements of the cause of action are disputed. The Rule cites the Restatement, UCC, or leading cases. The Application weighs the evidence against each element.
