ABOUT THIS TOOL

What is the Case Study Analyzer?

Case study analysis applies theoretical frameworks to real-world scenarios to extract generalizable lessons. Business case studies use frameworks like Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, or McKinsey's 7S. Legal case studies use IRAC or comparative precedent analysis. The goal is not merely to describe what happened but to diagnose why, what principles apply, and what decisions should follow.

When to use it: Use for MBA coursework, business school applications, professional training, legal education, and policy analysis.

HOW TO USE

How to Use the Case Study Analyzer

01

Select your case study type

Choose Business Strategy, Legal, Medical/Clinical, Educational, or Policy case study analysis.

02

Enter the case scenario

Describe the situation, the key decision points, the stakeholders involved, and the outcome (if known).

03

Choose your analytical framework

Select SWOT, PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, IRAC, Cost-Benefit, or Root Cause Analysis.

04

Generate recommendations

The analysis should conclude with actionable, evidence-based recommendations tied directly to the findings.

Pro tip: The weakest case study analyses describe the situation thoroughly but produce generic recommendations ("the company should improve communication"). Strong analysis ties each recommendation to a specific finding: "Because the 2023 supply chain disruption exposed single-supplier dependency in three product categories, management should immediately begin dual-sourcing for those categories."

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most common mistake in case study analysis?+
Describing the situation instead of analyzing it. Strong case study analysis spends at most 20% describing the situation and 80% analyzing, applying frameworks, and recommending. A case study is not a report on what happened — it is a structured analysis of why it happened and what should follow.
Which analytical framework should I use?+
Porter's Five Forces for industry competitiveness; SWOT for strategic position; PESTLE for macro-environment; McKinsey 7S for organizational alignment; Value Chain for operational efficiency. Many strong analyses combine multiple frameworks — SWOT to identify issues, Porter's to understand competitive dynamics, then strategic recommendations.
How long should recommendations be?+
Recommendations should be the most detailed section — typically 30-40% of the total analysis. Each recommendation should state: (1) what to do; (2) why (tied to a specific finding); (3) how to implement; (4) what success looks like.
