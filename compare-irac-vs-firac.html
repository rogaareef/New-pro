IRAC vs FIRAC vs CREAC: Which Legal Framework to Use?

The three major legal analysis frameworks — IRAC, FIRAC, and CREAC — serve different purposes. This guide explains exactly when to use each, with worked examples across US, UK, and international law.

⚡ Quick Reference

FrameworkStructureBest ForCommon In
IRACIssue→Rule→Application→ConclusionExams, quick analysisUS law schools, bar exam
FIRACFacts→Issue→Rule→Application→ConclusionComplex cases, briefsUK, Commonwealth, litigation
CREACConclusion→Rule→Explanation→Application→ConclusionLegal memos, court briefsUS legal writing programs

📋 When to Use IRAC

Use IRAC when: you are writing a law school exam answer, doing a quick legal analysis, or the facts are straightforward and uncontested. IRAC is the most commonly taught framework in US law schools and is the primary structure tested in bar exams (MBE, MEE, MPT).

Issue: Whether defendant breached duty of care...
Rule: Negligence requires: duty, breach, causation, damages...
Application: Here, defendant owed plaintiff a duty because...
Conclusion: Defendant is likely liable for negligence because...

📋 When to Use FIRAC

Use FIRAC when: the facts are complex, disputed, or central to the analysis. FIRAC is preferred in UK and Commonwealth law schools, and in complex litigation where detailed fact analysis is essential before legal analysis can proceed.

Facts: On January 1, defendant drove at 80mph in a 40mph zone...
Issue: Whether defendant's speed constituted negligence per se...
Rule: Negligence per se applies when defendant violates a statute...
Application: Defendant exceeded the speed limit by 100%...
Conclusion: Defendant is liable under negligence per se doctrine...

📋 When to Use CREAC

Use CREAC when: writing a legal memo, appellate brief, or any persuasive legal document. CREAC opens with your conclusion — immediately signaling your argument to the reader — which is preferred in professional legal writing.
